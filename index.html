QUANTITATIVE RESEARCH
"Usability" of Juror Instructions

CHALLENGE

The US legal system operates on the assumption that jurors are fully able to understand and apply burden of proof instructions ("by a preponderance of the evidence" (PoE) in civil trials, "beyond a reasonable doubt" (BRD) in criminal), but there is extensive evidence that people find these instructions confusing and do not apply them as intended by the law. The goal of this project was to identify sources of confusion in understanding such instructions in order to develop actionable recommendations for the reform of the language used in juror instructions.

MY ROLE

Lead Researcher (2017-2021)

I led multiple research projects on a team of behavioral researchers and legal experts

PROCESS

Comparing different instructions to determine which are applied successfully by laypeople (i.e. as intended by the law)

I conducted a series of experiments using a psychometric approach to quantify decision thresholds, which allowed me to statistically compare different instructions to each other and to the accepted legal thresholds for the PoE (50%) and BRD (90%) burdens. The different instructions tested for each burden were selected based on previous qualitative research that identified potential sources of confusion reported by jurors. I implemented my research using the Qualtrics survey platform, with thousands of participants recruited from across the US via Amazon Mechanical Turk and email.

Together my experiments yielded a number of important findings:

  • The phrase "preponderance of the evidence" itself biases individuals to be significantly more stringent than intended by the law regardless of the definition (or lack thereof) provided, but only when the phrase is used in an explicitly legal context
  • Participants' decision thresholds were consistent with the prescribed PoE standard when the instruction used an equivalent but more familiar term instead (e.g. "more likely true than not", "greater than 50%")
  • Participants who received no instruction whatsoever had decision thresholds around 50%, consistent with the prescribed PoE standard
  • Defining "reasonable doubt" biases individuals to be significantly more lenient than intended by the law, compared to when no definition is included in the instruction
  • Participants' decision thresholds were consistent with the prescribed BRD standard when the instruction did not define "reasonable doubt" and when the instruction was "beyond any doubt"
  • Using equivalent but more familiar terms instead of BRD (e.g. "firmly convinced", "greater than 90%") made decision thresholds less consistent with the prescribed BRD standard
  • No instruction led to decision thresholds that were significantly more stringent than the prescribed BRD standard

Key insights and recommendations for improving juror burden of proof instructions

Because we identified distinct factors contributing to laypeople's misapplication of PoE versus BRD, we developed actionable recommendations for improving instruction language that were specific to each legal burden of proof:

  • PoE instructions should leverage the fact that individuals default to a 50% decision threshold (equal to the prescribed PoE standard) in the absence of any instruction by reducing the biasing effects of the instructions themselves
  • PoE instructions for civil trials should avoid unfamiliar legalese language (i.e. "preponderance of the evidence") in favor of equivalent but simplified language such as "more likely true than not"
  • BRD definitions focus on preventing jurors from getting carried away with imagined doubts, but this has the unintended consequence of causing people to dismiss even reasonable doubts, leading to more lenient decision thresholds and more false convictions
  • BRD instructions for criminal trials should avoid any language that seeks to define or limit jurors' "reasonable doubt"
  • BRD instructions should be phrased to clearly establish that the burden is on the government to provide sufficient proof that leaves no reasonable doubt of guilt, not on the defendant to provide sufficient doubt

OUTCOME

We are working to share our findings and recommendations to impact the legal system via publication in legal journals and articles targeted to educate legal professionals and policy makers; I am responsible for preparing all reports, figures, and manuscripts for publication

These findings will also be shared broadly to reach a wide audience, through diverse media outlets that have disseminated work by this team in the past, including the New York Times Sunday Magazine, The Wall Street Journal, Scientific American, National Public Radio, and the Public Broadcasting Service

WEBSITE REDESIGN
Elemental Brass

CHALLENGE

Elemental Brass is a family-run small business providing products and services for French horns. The existing website had usability issues that caused problems for customers. The goal for this project was to redesign the entire website with a user-centered focus.

MY ROLE

UX Research & Design (2020)

I worked as part of a team that included the business owner and staff responsible for marketing

PROCESS

Assessing trends and problems with the existing website

Reviewing website analytics (for 2019) yielded several important findings:

  • The site Home page had a bounce rate of 50%
  • Only 10% of visitors viewed at least one product during their visit
  • The majority of site visits (60%) were made from mobile devices

Interviews and baseline usability testing indicated that users:

  • Could not immediately identify where products were located
  • Were frustrated navigating between different site pages
  • Were overwhelmed by text blocks
  • Struggled to use the mobile site

Key insights and recommendations that I developed and implemented

I developed the following recommendations as the basis for the redesign:

  • Add navigation bar with descriptive labels so users can easily navigate the site, and for search engine optimization
  • Create product categories to allow users to quickly browse and filter to find what they are looking for and compare similar products; use categories and sales trends to display recommended products on product pages
  • Simplify site design and limit use of text blocks to reduce visual clutter
  • Ensure site features are displaying as intended on a variety of desktop and mobile devices
  • Use consistent logos and design across the entire website

We centered users in the process of planning the website's organization:

  • We generated lists of potential descriptive labels/product categories, consisting of top search terms that brought users to the site and examples from several other instrument websites
  • A user (French horn player) completed an open card sort of products within each broad project category to create and name subcategories
  • A second user had a nearly identical closed card sort of products into these subcategories
  • I completed several iterations of user feedback and changes (interviews, usability testing) using an offline version of the site as an interactive prototype

OUTCOME

Feedback from the French horn community was positive and customers indicated that the new website was easy to navigate

The improved user experience was also reflected in site analytics:

  • The website saw a 609% increase in unique visitors for 2020 vs 2019
  • The bounce rate for the Home page decreased to 36%
  • 43% of visitors viewed at least one product during their visit, which further translated into a 300% increased conversion rate for 2020 vs 2019
  • 55% of online orders for 2020 came from organic searches, indicating that the new descriptive labels and product categories successfully increased Elemental's Brass visibility

QUALITATIVE RESEARCH
Melanoma Prevention

CHALLENGE

A melanoma memorial foundation wanted research to assess strategies to improve sun protection among adolescents. The goal of this project was to develop actionable recommendations for media interventions targeted at reducing melanoma rates in Colorado.

MY ROLE

Research Assistant (2012-2014)

I worked with a team of behavioral researchers and people impacted by melanoma

PROCESS

Conducting focus group interviews with 6th-8th graders

We conducted six focus groups with Colorado students, which I transcribed and coded to identify themes that yielded important findings:

  • Participants with friends or relatives who had a skin cancer diagnosis were aware of the importance of sun protection
  • Participants reported limited education about the dangers of skin cancer
  • Factors that facilitate sun protection include remembering prior sunburns, avoiding skin cancer, and reminders
  • Barriers to engaging in sun protection include forgetting, physical discomfort, inconvenience, desire to tan, and lack of perceived effectiveness or need
  • There were misconceptions about effective protective practices and participants were uncertain or had inaccurate information about skin cancer

Key insights and recommendations for media promoting sun protection

We developed the following actionable recommendations for education-based interventions:

  • Emphasize the importance multiple methods of sun protection, in addition to sunscreen (e.g. clothing, shade)
  • Target perceived susceptibility by teaching about the negative consequences of sun exposure and the impact it may have on their lives
  • Provide consistent environmental cues (e.g. sunscreen dispensers in locker rooms) to prompt protective behavior
  • Ensure interventions are relevant to all members of the target audience
  • Emphasize the benefit of acting now and encourage engagement through interactive learning experiences
  • Acknowledge that the importance of physical attractiveness for adolescents increases their desire to tan, and use this value to also enhance sun protection by including information about cosmetic damage associated with sun exposure
  • Apply school-based interventions to promote sun protection as a social norm and group behavior

OUTCOME

Our findings and recommendations were shared publicly through presentation at a regional conference and publication in the Journal of Primary Prevention; I presented the work and contributed to writing the manuscript

We also reported directly to stakeholders at the memorial foundation who had funded the research, and they used our recommendations to update an educational video aimed at increasing sun protective behavior in adolescents
